**Introduction:**

This lecture explores the concept of *Nishkama Karma* (selfless action) as presented in the Bhagavad Gita.  The lecture examines its religious, social, and philosophical implications, emphasizing its role in breaking the cycle of birth and rebirth.  The Bhagavad Gita, a central text of Hinduism, serves as the primary source for understanding this complex philosophical idea.

**Section 1: The Bhagavad Gita and the Context of Nishkama Karma** [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=38)

*Summary:* This section introduces the Bhagavad Gita, its cultural significance, and the setting where the concept of Nishkama Karma is introduced: the dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna on the Kurukshetra battlefield.

*Key Points:*

* The Bhagavad Gita is a revered Hindu scripture, part of the Mahabharata epic. It is considered a source of profound wisdom about the nature of humanity and divinity. [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=76)
* The central narrative depicts a philosophical dialogue between Lord Krishna and Arjuna, a warrior facing a moral dilemma on the battlefield. [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=108)
* Arjuna's reluctance to fight his kinsmen prompts Krishna to expound on *Nishkama Karma*, emphasizing the importance of acting out of duty without attachment to results.
> "The moral worth of an action lies in the motive, not the consequence." [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=266)

* The Gita asserts that every action (karma) has consequences, but the timing of these consequences can vary.


**Section 2: Types of Karma and the Cycle of Birth and Rebirth** [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=513)

*Summary:* This section categorizes karma and explains how actions performed with desire (Samskrita Karma) bind the soul to the cycle of birth and rebirth, while selfless actions (Nishkama Karma) offer a path to liberation.

*Key Points:*

* The Gita classifies karma into three types: [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=1744)
    * **Karma:** Actions performed according to prescribed duties. [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=571)
    * **Akarma:** Non-action or inaction. [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=1120)
[slide 3]
    * **Vikarma:** Actions performed through misuse of freedom, leading to negative consequences. [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=664)
* Karma is also categorized based on desire: [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=425)
    * **Samskrita Karma:** Actions performed with desire for results, leading to attachment and bondage.  These actions fuel the cycle of birth and death.
    * **Nishkama Karma:** Actions performed without desire for results, motivated solely by duty. [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=571)
> "Bondage is not a quality of karma itself, but a mental attitude of attachment." [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=953)
* Attachment and detachment are mental states, not inherent properties of actions themselves.
* *Asakta Karma*, actions performed with desire, lack moral worth and contribute to bondage. [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=571)

**Section 3: Understanding Nishkama Karma** [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=1166)

[slide 4]
*Summary:* This section defines Nishkama Karma, clarifies its implications, and contrasts it with actions motivated by desire.

*Key Points:*

* *Nishkama* means "without desire," and *Karma* means "action."  Therefore, *Nishkama Karma* is selfless action performed without expectation of reward.
* It emphasizes acting out of duty and moral obligation, detached from personal gain or loss.
> "Krishna tells Arjuna his right lies in Karma, not its result. One does not control the fruits of Karma." [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=1719)
* The Gita advocates for the renunciation of the *fruits* of karma, not the renunciation of karma itself.  All beings perform actions; even breathing is an action.
* Human existence is inherently active; inaction is not a viable path to liberation. [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=1509)

**Section 4:  Nishkama Karma and Self-Realization** [ðŸ”‰](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8YntdSQijAY?t=1126)

*Summary:* This section discusses the characteristics of a self-realized individual and how their actions naturally align with Nishkama Karma.

*Key Points:*

* A self-realized person, acting consistently according to moral duty, embodies *Nishkama Karma*.  Their actions lead to liberation.
* Gradual control over the senses is crucial in achieving this state. This is difficult for ordinary people due to ingrained habits and laziness.
* Example: Two men save a drowning girl. One acts out of desire (Samskrita Karma), becoming bound by karma; the other acts out of duty (Nishkama Karma), remaining free from karmic bondage.

**Conclusion:**

The Bhagavad Gita's message is not about inaction but about acting selflessly, without attachment to the outcomes of one's actions.  *Nishkama Karma* is a path towards liberation and self-realization, a journey that requires understanding, practice, and the gradual control of the senses.
