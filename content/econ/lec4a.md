**2. Elasticity of Demand**

* **Key Idea:** The elasticity of demand measures how responsive the quantity demanded is to changes in price.  [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=668)

* **Formula:** Epsilon (Îµ) = (*Î”Q*/ *Q*) / (*Î”P*/ *P*). This formula calculates the percentage change in quantity demanded for a percentage change in price. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=678)

* **Interpretation:**

   * **Negative Values:** Epsilon is always negative for downward-sloping demand curves because price and quantity move in opposite directions. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=690)
   * **Magnitude:** The larger the absolute value of epsilon, the more elastic the demand. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1290)

* **Extremes:**

   * **Perfectly Inelastic Demand (Îµ = 0):**  In this case, the quantity demanded does not change regardless of price changes. Examples include essential goods like insulin or water, where there are no substitutes.
   * **Perfectly Elastic Demand (Îµ = -âˆž):** In this case, any price increase results in a complete loss of demand, and any price decrease leads to unlimited demand. Examples include goods with perfect substitutes, such as different brands of gum or fast food.

* **Visual Representation:**

   * **Figure 4.3:**  A perfectly elastic demand curve is horizontal, indicating that price does not change, while a perfectly inelastic demand curve is vertical, meaning the quantity demanded remains constant regardless of price.

## Consumer Choice: Income Shifts and Elasticity [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1414)

### Summary

This section explores how changes in income affect demand curves and introduces the concept of the Engel curve. It also delves into the classification of goods based on income elasticity, including normal, inferior, luxury, and necessities. The lecture then transitions to analyzing the effects of price changes, decomposing them into substitution and income effects.

### Income Shifts and the Engel Curve [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1177)

![Slide 5](/jott/econ/lec4/slide_5.png#center)
- **Income Changes and Demand:** When income increases, consumers generally choose to consume more of both goods. Conversely, when income decreases, they consume less of both goods. 
> "As your income goes up, you will choose more of both. As your income goes down, you will choose less of both." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1246)

- **Engel Curve:** The Engel curve represents the relationship between income and the quantity demanded of a specific good. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1268)
> "The Engel curve is the relationship between income and quantity demanded." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1268)

- **Income Elasticity of Demand:** The slope of the Engel curve is known as the income elasticity of demand, denoted by gamma (*Î³*). It measures the percentage change in quantity demanded in response to a percentage change in income.
> "Gamma, which is *delta Q* over *Q*, *Q*0, once again, if we're doing just greatly. Over *delta y* over *y*0. That's the income elasticity of demand." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1299)

- **Linear vs. Constant Elasticity Curves:** The Engel curve in the example is linear, but in general, it can be non-linear. Real-world demand curves are often approximated as locally linear, which is a simplification. 
> "We know a constant elasticity curve will not be linear. Okay? If it's linear, it's not constant elasticity because if it's linear, the elasticity will change along the curve." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1336)

### Classifying Goods Based on Income Elasticity [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1414)

- **Normal Goods:** These goods have a positive income elasticity (*Î³* > 0), meaning that as income increases, the quantity demanded also increases. 
> "We call goods with a positive income elasticity, normal goods." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1417)

- **Inferior Goods:** These goods have a negative income elasticity (*Î³* < 0), meaning that as income increases, the quantity demanded decreases.
> "We call those inferior goods." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1439)

- **Examples:** Fast food, potatoes (in the past). [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2453)
> "Literally, richer people may probably eat less fast food than poorer people." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1507)
> "We are like, in the old days, like the four fast food, like that was the cheap, filling, city-tasting food stuff that guys sort of used to eat all the time." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1538)

- **Luxury Goods:** These goods have an income elasticity greater than one (*Î³* > 1), meaning that as income increases, the quantity demanded increases proportionally more than the income change.
> "These are going to be goods where gamma is greater than one and necessities, gamma is less than one." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1572)

- **Examples:** Watches, boats, luxury brands.
![Slide 6](/jott/econ/lec4/slide_6.png#center)
> "In theory, necessities are things like food where clearly rich people spend more on food than poor people." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1605)

- **Necessities:** These goods have an income elasticity less than one (*Î³* < 1), meaning that as income increases, the quantity demanded increases proportionally less than the income change.
> "The richer you get, the more of your budget you spend on it. In theory, necessities are things like food where clearly rich people spend more on food than poor people." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1605)

### Price Changes: Substitution and Income Effects [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2605)

- **Decomposing Price Change Effects:** The lecture introduces the concept of decomposing the effect of a price change into two components: the substitution effect and the income effect. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2488)
> "The way we are going to do that is that we are going to decompose your response to a price change into two effects: the substitution effect and the income effect." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1752)

- **Substitution Effect:** The substitution effect is the change in the quantity demanded of a good when the price changes, holding utility constant.  [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1782)
> "The substitution effect, we are going to define the substitution effect as the change in the quantity of a good when the price changes holding utility constant." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1775)
    - **Compensated Demand:** The substitution effect is also known as compensated demand because it represents the change in quantity demanded when the consumer is compensated to maintain their original utility level.
    > "We call this notion compensated demand. Compensated demand. That is, I am compensating you." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2008)
    - **Always Negative:** The substitution effect is always negative. This is because when the price of a good increases, consumers will always substitute towards cheaper alternatives, leading to a decrease in the quantity demanded of the good whose price increased.
    > "The substitution effect is always negative. We can see this in two ways." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2049)

- **Income Effect:** The income effect is the change in the quantity demanded of a good due to a change in purchasing power, which is effectively a change in income. This effect arises because the price change affects the consumer's ability to purchase goods.
> "The income effect is the change in the quantity of a good as income changes." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1806)

- **Graphical Representation:** The lecture uses a graphical representation to illustrate the substitution and income effects. The budget constraint shifts due to the price change, and an imaginary budget constraint is drawn to isolate the substitution effect.
> "We can exactly represent that by the shift from BC prime to BC 2. Because BC prime and BC 2 have the same slope. That shift is just the income effect." [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2242)

###  Income and Substitution Effects [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2824)

- **Substitution Effect:**  When the price of a good increases, consumers substitute away from that good and towards other cheaper alternatives. This always works in the opposite direction of the price change.
- **Income Effect:** When the price of a good changes, it affects the consumer's purchasing power, making them effectively richer or poorer. This effect can work in the same direction as the substitution effect (for normal goods) or in the opposite direction (for inferior goods).

### Inferior Goods

- An **inferior good** is a good where the demand decreases as income increases. This is opposite to a **normal good**, where demand increases with income.
- Example: **Steak and Potatoes** [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1535)
    - Initial budget constraint: Price of steak = $5, price of potatoes = $1, income = $25. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=408)
    - Consumer chooses point A, consuming 7.5 potatoes and 3.5 steaks. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2382)
    - Price of potatoes increases to $3. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2465)
    - **Substitution Effect:**  Consumer moves from point A to point B, consuming fewer potatoes (4) due to the higher price. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=1775)
    - **Income Effect:**  Because potatoes are inferior, the consumer now wants more potatoes as they are effectively poorer. They move back from point B to point C, consuming 5 potatoes.
    - **Net Effect:** The consumer consumes fewer potatoes, but the substitution and income effects work in opposite directions. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2438)

> *"The income effect always goes the same way as the substitution effect. This is sort of just a purely useless theoretical exercise. It gets interesting when the income effect goes the opposite way of the substitution effect."* [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2488)

- **Utility Function:** The type of utility function determines whether a good is normal or inferior. A different utility function is needed to create an inferior good. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2505)

### Giffen Goods

- **Giffen Goods:**  Goods that exhibit an upward-sloping demand curve, meaning a higher price leads to an increased demand. This is a rare occurrence, where the income effect overwhelms the substitution effect.
- **Theoretical Concept:**  Giffen goods are a theoretical concept, and finding real-world examples is challenging. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=47)
- **Evidence:**  A study in China provided some evidence for the existence of Giffen goods. Very poor households that primarily consumed rice were given coupons to reduce the price of rice. 
    - **Non-superpoor households:**  Exhibited a typical downward-sloping demand curve, buying more rice with the discount. [ðŸ”‰](https://youtu.be/x0scPosOsoI?t=2896)
    - **Superpoor households:**  Experienced an upward-sloping demand curve, buying less rice due to the discount. This is because the income effect outweighed the substitution effect. They used the extra money from the discount to buy other goods.
- **Corner Solution:** Giffen goods can be considered a corner solution where consumers allocate their entire budget to a single good.
