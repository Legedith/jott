2. Elasticity of Demand

  • Key Idea: The elasticity of demand measures how responsive the quantity demanded is to changes in price. ğŸ”‰

  • Formula: Epsilon (Îµ) = (Î”Q/ Q) / (Î”P/ P). This formula calculates the percentage change in quantity demanded for a percentage change in price. ğŸ”‰

  • Interpretation:

    • Negative Values: Epsilon is always negative for downward-sloping demand curves because price and quantity move in opposite directions. ğŸ”‰
    • Magnitude: The larger the absolute value of epsilon, the more elastic the demand. ğŸ”‰

  • Extremes:

    • Perfectly Inelastic Demand (Îµ = 0): In this case, the quantity demanded does not change regardless of price changes. Examples include essential goods like insulin or water, where there are no substitutes.

    • Perfectly Elastic Demand (Îµ = -âˆ): In this case, any price increase results in a complete loss of demand, and any price decrease leads to unlimited demand. Examples include goods with perfect substitutes, such as different brands of gum or fast food.

A perfectly elastic demand curve is horizontal, indicating that price does not change, while a perfectly inelastic demand curve is vertical, meaning the quantity demanded remains constant regardless of price.

Income and Substitution Effects ğŸ”‰#

  • Substitution Effect: When the price of a good increases, consumers substitute away from that good and towards other cheaper alternatives. This always works in the opposite direction of the price change.
  • Income Effect: When the price of a good changes, it affects the consumer’s purchasing power, making them effectively richer or poorer. This effect can work in the same direction as the substitution effect (for normal goods) or in the opposite direction (for inferior goods).

Inferior Goods#

  • An inferior good is a good where the demand decreases as income increases. This is opposite to a normal good, where demand increases with income.
  • Example: Steak and Potatoes ğŸ”‰
    • Initial budget constraint: Price of steak = $5, price of potatoes = $1, income = $25. ğŸ”‰
    • Consumer chooses point A, consuming 7.5 potatoes and 3.5 steaks. ğŸ”‰
    • Price of potatoes increases to $3. ğŸ”‰
    • Substitution Effect: Consumer moves from point A to point B, consuming fewer potatoes (4) due to the higher price. ğŸ”‰
    • Income Effect: Because potatoes are inferior, the consumer now wants more potatoes as they are effectively poorer. They move back from point B to point C, consuming 5 potatoes.
    • Net Effect: The consumer consumes fewer potatoes, but the substitution and income effects work in opposite directions. ğŸ”‰

“If the income effect always goes the same way as the substitution effect. This is sort of just a purely useless theoretical exercise. It gets interesting when the income effect goes the opposite way of the substitution effect.” ğŸ”‰

  • Utility Function: The type of utility function determines whether a good is normal or inferior. A different utility function is needed to create an inferior good. ğŸ”‰

Giffen Goods#

  • Giffen Goods: Goods that exhibit an upward-sloping demand curve, meaning a higher price leads to an increased demand. This is a rare occurrence, where the income effect overwhelms the substitution effect.
  • Theoretical Concept: Giffen goods are a theoretical concept, and finding real-world examples is challenging. ğŸ”‰
  • Evidence: A study in China provided some evidence for the existence of Giffen goods. Very poor households that primarily consumed rice were given coupons to reduce the price of rice.
    • Non-superpoor households: Exhibited a typical downward-sloping demand curve, buying more rice with the discount. ğŸ”‰
    • Superpoor households: Experienced an upward-sloping demand curve, buying less rice due to the discount. This is because the income effect outweighed the substitution effect. They used the extra money from the discount to buy other goods.
  • Corner Solution: Giffen goods can be considered a corner solution where consumers allocate their entire budget to a single good.

Key Takeaways#

  • The income effect can work in the opposite direction of the substitution effect for inferior goods. ğŸ”‰
  • Giffen goods are theoretical goods where the income effect dominates the substitution effect, leading to an upward-sloping demand curve. ğŸ”‰
  • Real-world examples of Giffen goods are extremely rare.
  • Understanding the interaction between income and substitution effects is crucial for analyzing consumer behavior and market dynamics. ğŸ”‰
